## 0️⃣ Adam Brown + Dwarkesh Patel on How Einstein Derived General Relativity

<br>

First, Dwarkesh Patel invites Adam Brown for a [blackboard lecture on how General Relativity was developed by Einstein](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QbdbAhaJoCQ).

Adam, a theoretical physicist at Google DeepMind and a very talented lecturer, starts with the origins of the theory by comparing Newtonian physics with Special Relativity, tracing the path that led Einstein to General Relativity from principles such as the equivalence between gravitational and inertial mass.

With questions such as, *"could be the case that gravity mass is the same as the inertial force?"*, he ventures into a quick experimental demonstration with the classic spinning bucket experiment. He then turns his attention to the curvature of spacetime and its relationship to matter, and, through the tensorial generalization of mass and energy, derives the Einstein field equations.

Next comes a discussion of black holes, their inevitable existence as predicted by General Relativity, and the contrast they present compared with Special Relativity. Starting from the beautiful Schwarzschild metric, which describes the gravitational field around a spherically symmetric mass, they derive the Schwarzschild radius and discuss its asymptotic behavior, providing intuition for what lies inside these extraordinary objects (i.e., the singularity).

Gravity around a black hole can be understood as a Taylor expansion correction to the familiar Newtonian gravitational field (first order), and Adam goes into detail about how the equations behave both near and inside the event horizon.

In terms of energy generation, while fusion and fission rely on the strong and weak forces, neither fundamentally converts the rest mass of matter with the efficiency that gravity could. Chemical and nuclear reactions tap only a small fraction of the energy stored in mass, whereas accretion onto black holes could convert a much larger fraction into radiation (in theory).

*Adam and Dwarkesh end the episode by wondering about the future of physics in the age of AI, leaving physicists and philosophers alike with a marvelous sense of curiosity. Thank you, folks!*

## 1️⃣ Brian Greene + Claudia de Rham on Einstein's Theory 2.0

<br>

Beauty, beauty, beauty.

Brian Greene invites Claudia de Rham from Imperial College London to discuss [the sublime elegance of understanding theoretical, geometrical, and mathematical descriptions of the deepest fabric of spacetime](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ypRYKVI4VUc).

Claudia likes to draw analogies between the phenomenological descriptions of electromagnetism and gravity, focusing on the fundamental symmetries of the underlying fields. Her theory of gravity revolves around the possibility that the graviton may possess an incredibly tiny — but nonzero — mass.

They also revisit one of the most fundamental assumptions of relativity. Although experiments place extraordinarily tight constraints on the photon's mass, are we really, really certain that it is exactly zero?

They discuss the parallel efforts of mathematical physicists and particle theorists to understand gravity and the graviton. The conversation naturally moves to gravitational waves — which even Einstein initially doubted existed — black holes, the cosmological constant, and the broader implications for modern field theory.

Is the universality of gravity coming to an end?

*Great conversation, folks — I can't wait to see what comes next in this fundamental discussion.*
